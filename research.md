# What Most Improves Two-Sided Match Quality in High-Volume Frontline Hiring

## TL;DR
- The single highest-leverage, lowest-risk intervention is **candidate-side self-selection driven by honest, specific, balanced job information (Realistic Job Previews) combined with a small number of decision-relevant self-qualification questions on the three axes that actually predict frontline outcomes: availability, genuine intent, and role/schedule fit, not formal experience.** This works through the causal mechanism with the cleanest evidence in the literature: previews raise perceived employer honesty and let poorly-matched applicants screen themselves out before they consume recruiter time, while suitable applicants stay.
- The recruiter-side automation where most commercial tools have converged (conversational pre-screening, auto-scheduling: Paradox/Olivia, Fountain, Phenom) demonstrably improves **throughput and speed**, but there is little independent evidence it improves **two-sided match quality**; and pure algorithmic ranking carries the worst combined risk of false negatives, disparate impact, and regulatory exposure.
- Treating the problem as a two-sided matching market rather than a one-way filter is the correct framing: the binding constraint in frontline hiring is not a shortage of resumes but mismatched preferences and information (availability, schedule, location, pay transparency, speed) and mid-funnel withdrawal/ghosting. The best design reduces wasted effort on BOTH sides by surfacing dealbreakers early and honestly, not by adding generic friction or by automating rejection.

## Key Findings

**1. Locus of leverage is the candidate side, not the recruiter side.** Most commercial tooling has converged on faster recruiter-side triage, but the cleanest causal evidence for improving match quality (as opposed to speed) sits with candidate self-selection. The recruiter-side convergence is best understood as evidence of what is easy to sell and measure (time-to-hire, recruiter hours), not as evidence of where match-quality gains are largest.

**2. "Unsuitable" decomposes into three axes that are not equally predictive.** Not-qualified, not-available, and not-interested require different instruments. For frontline/hourly roles, formal qualification and prior experience are weak predictors of performance and retention, while availability, schedule fit, and genuine intent matter more. Van Iddekinge et al. (2019, Personnel Psychology, 81 samples) found prehire work experience correlates only .06 with job performance, .11 with training performance, and .00 with turnover. Conscientiousness is the most robust dispositional predictor and is strongest precisely in routinized frontline work (Shaffer & Postlethwaite, 2013).

**3. Realistic Job Previews (RJPs) have modest but real and causally clean effects.** Four meta-analyses (Premack & Wanous 1985; McEvoy & Cascio 1985; Phillips 1998; Earnest, Allen & Landis 2011) converge: RJPs reduce turnover and attrition with small effect sizes (Earnest et al. report r about -.07 with voluntary turnover across k=52, N about 17,000). The dominant mechanism in the most recent meta-analytic path analysis is **perceived organizational honesty**, not met-expectations or role clarity. Self-selection is one of several proposed mechanisms.

**4. Friction is a double-edged signal.** Spence (1973) job-market signalling and search-and-matching theory (Diamond-Mortensen-Pissarides) explain why application cost can separate high- from low-intent applicants, but only when the cost is differentially cheaper for genuine/able candidates. Generic friction (long forms, account creation) deters strong candidates who have options without selecting on quality. The calibration point is friction that is decision-relevant (does this person actually want THIS shift pattern at THIS location?) rather than effort-for-its-own-sake.

**5. Algorithmic ranking scales but carries the worst risk profile.** Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) established large baseline human bias (white-named resumes got 50% more callbacks). Modern LLM screeners reproduce and sometimes invert these biases: An et al. (2025, PNAS Nexus) found leading LLMs systematically favored female candidates and disadvantaged Black male applicants with identical qualifications. Fabris et al. (2024/2025, ACM TIST) conclude it remains unproven whether algorithmic hiring is less biased than low-tech alternatives. Automated screening also generates invisible false negatives (qualified candidates silently rejected).

**6. The matching-market framing reveals the real bottleneck.** Groh et al. (2015, IZA Journal of Labor Economics) ran a randomized matching experiment in Jordan: even after making over 1,000 educationally-screened matches, youth rejected interviews 28% of the time and rejected/quickly quit offers 83% of the time, because of preferences over non-wage job attributes. The lesson: removing search friction without addressing preference/fit produces inefficiency on both sides. In frontline hiring, mid-funnel drop-off and ghosting are dominant: per the iCIMS 2025 State of Frontline Hiring Report (surveying 1,000 frontline workers and 1,000 hiring managers), hospitality has the highest abandonment of any frontline sector at 68%, 16 points above healthcare's 52%, and most abandonment is driven by application length and uncertainty about qualifications.

**7. Candidate experience and equity are hard constraints, with measurable guardrails.** Gilliland's (1993) procedural-justice framework and the Hausknecht et al. (2004) applicant-reactions meta-analysis define the fairness rules (job-relatedness, opportunity to perform, transparency, consistency). Success metrics should be two-sided: recruiter hours per hire, qualified-applicant yield, abandonment/drop-off rate, time-to-hire, perceived procedural fairness, and adverse-impact (four-fifths) ratios.

**8. The regulatory envelope bounds the solution space and favors self-selection.** The EU AI Act classifies recruitment/screening AI as high-risk (Annex III point 4), with obligations enforceable 2 August 2026. NYC Local Law 144 mandates annual independent bias audits and candidate notice for automated employment decision tools. In the UK, UK GDPR Article 22 (now replaced by Articles 22A-D via the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025) restricts solely-automated significant decisions, and the Equality Act 2010 (s.19 indirect discrimination) catches disparate-impact screens. The ICO's November 2024 audit of AI recruitment tools made almost 300 recommendations and flagged fairness, inferred special-category data, and accuracy failures. Honest self-selection and transparent self-reported eligibility questions sit comfortably inside this envelope; opaque algorithmic ranking sits at its riskiest edge.

## Details

### Sub-question 1: Where is the leverage, candidate, recruiter, or algorithm?

The commercial market has overwhelmingly invested in recruiter-side triage. Paradox's Olivia automates screening, knockout questions, and scheduling; vendor case studies report large operational gains (per the Paradox/Workday "Ava Cado" case study from February 2025, Chipotle cut time from application to "apron on" from 12 days to 4 days, raised application completion from 50% to over 85%, and doubled applications). But these are throughput, completion, and speed metrics, not selection-validity metrics, and they are vendor-reported. Independent reviewers note "almost no publicly available case studies with hard numbers" on match-quality improvement, and that Paradox "is built to compress time to first interview" rather than to improve selection validity.

The evidence base for actually improving the quality of the match (reducing both wasted recruiter review and wasted candidate effort) is strongest on the candidate side, because that is where the asymmetric information sits: the candidate knows their own availability, intent, and constraints far better than any resume parser can infer them. The economically efficient move is to elicit that private information honestly and early, which is a self-selection / signalling design, not a ranking problem.

Algorithmic surfacing (ranking the pool) is the weakest-evidence, highest-risk locus. A recent formalization of "algorithmic friction" frames automated screening as introducing a measurable false-negative rate that accumulates invisibly at scale, because rejected candidates get no feedback and firms see only who passed the filter.

### Sub-question 2: Decomposing "unsuitable"

The three axes:
- **Not qualified.** For frontline/hourly roles this is the least predictive axis. Van Iddekinge et al. (2019) is decisive: prehire experience essentially does not predict performance (.06) or turnover (.00). The Sackett et al. (2022) re-estimation of selection validity also downgraded general mental ability (from about .51 to about .31 operational validity) and ranked structured interviews and work samples (job-specific, behavior-based) above generic credentials. Education and years of experience sit near the bottom of the validity hierarchy.
- **Not available / wrong schedule fit.** This is underweighted in the literature but is the dominant practical dealbreaker in shift-based work. The Groh et al. (2015) result, where 83% of made matches were rejected or quickly abandoned over non-wage attributes, is the strongest experimental evidence that availability/preference fit dominates formal qualification in determining whether a match survives.
- **Not interested / low intent.** Captured by signalling theory and by the candidate-experience data on ghosting and abandonment. Genuine intent is what friction can (carefully) screen for.

The dispositional predictors that DO matter for frontline outcomes are conscientiousness (strongest in routinized work; Shaffer & Postlethwaite 2013), emotional stability and agreeableness for turnover (Zimmerman 2008 meta-analytic path model: emotional stability best predicts intention to quit; conscientiousness and agreeableness best predict actual turnover), and empirically-keyed biodata (corrected validity about .44 for empirically-scored composites). Crucially these are all things better elicited via short structured self-report or situational-judgment items than inferred from a CV.

### Sub-question 3: The self-selection mechanism (RJPs)

The RJP literature is the heart of the answer. Four meta-analyses:
- **Premack & Wanous (1985, Journal of Applied Psychology):** RJPs lower initial expectations, increase self-selection, and modestly reduce turnover; about 74% of variance across criteria attributable to sampling error (i.e., effects are consistent but small).
- **Phillips (1998, Academy of Management Journal, 40 studies):** RJPs reduce attrition from recruitment, lower initial expectations, modestly raise performance and lower voluntary and overall turnover; medium of delivery and timing moderate effects.
- **Earnest, Allen & Landis (2011, Personnel Psychology, k=52, N about 17,000):** the definitive mechanism study. Of the candidate mechanisms (met expectations, role clarity, perceived honesty, self-selection, perceived fit), only **perceived organizational honesty** significantly mediated the RJP-to-voluntary-turnover path when all were modelled together. Voluntary turnover correlation about r = -.07.

The two distinct effects to keep separate:
- The **self-selection effect** (pre-hire): honest previews cause poorly-matched applicants to withdraw. This is the effect inploi most wants, because it reduces both recruiter review and candidate wasted effort.
- The **met-expectations / adjustment effect** (post-hire): survivors adjust better and quit less.

Signalling theory (the preview as a credible honesty signal), attraction-selection-attrition (Schneider's ASA: organizations and individuals converge through self-selection over time), and person-job/person-organization fit (Kristof-Brown et al. 2005 meta-analysis: P-J fit correlates about .47 with job satisfaction and negatively with intent to quit) all reinforce the same mechanism.

**Critical assessment of interactive vs static previews:** Nearly all the meta-analyzed studies tested static text, oral, or video previews. Earnest et al. found video RJPs produced higher applicant acceptance and higher perceived honesty than oral or written. There is a strong theoretical case (but thin direct experimental evidence) that **interactive previews that combine realistic content with personalized self-assessment** (e.g., "here is the actual shift pattern; can you work it?"; situational-judgment scenarios drawn from the real job) would produce a larger self-selection effect than passive previews, because they convert the preview from information into a decision. This is the most promising and least-tested design space, and it is exactly where a candidate-engagement layer like inploi could generate proprietary causal evidence via A/B testing.

### Sub-question 4: Friction as a signal, and the calibration point

Spence (1973): a signal separates types only when it is differentially costly, cheaper for the desired type. Education works as a signal because it is cheaper for high-ability people. Applied to applications: friction screens for intent only if the cost is lower for genuinely-interested/able candidates than for indifferent ones. A scheduling-availability question or a short job-relevant scenario is cheap for someone who genuinely wants and can do the job, and "expensive" (revealing) for someone who cannot or will not, so it separates on the right dimension. A long generic form or mandatory account creation is expensive for everyone, including strong candidates with outside options, so it deters quality (adverse selection against the firm).

The candidate-experience data quantify the deterrence side: per Appcast's 2025 Recruitment Marketing Benchmark Report, applications taking under 5 minutes convert at 12.47% versus just 3.61% for those over 15 minutes, a roughly 245% completion gap from length alone; and per Appcast, applications requiring account creation see 40% higher abandonment. Hospitality leads all frontline sectors in abandonment (68%, per iCIMS 2025). A theoretical matching-market caution (Weinberg & Zou, "Signal to noise in matching markets") shows that reducing application cost without an effective signalling mechanism can make both sides worse off, because the receiving side cannot learn true preferences. The calibration point: add only friction that carries decision-relevant information (availability, location, shift, genuine-interest confirmation, short SJT), and strip all friction that does not (redundant fields, logins, CV re-keying).

### Sub-question 5: Algorithmic ranking vs human triage

Human triage is biased: Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) is the canonical evidence (white names 50% more callbacks; equivalent to about 8 extra years of experience; callbacks also less responsive to resume quality for Black-named applicants). This is the case FOR automation.

But automation does not reliably remove bias and can add new bias plus opacity:
- **An et al. (2025, PNAS Nexus):** across GPT-3.5/4o, Gemini 1.5, Claude 3.5, Llama 3, LLMs systematically favored female candidates and disadvantaged Black male applicants at identical qualifications; intersectional effects on the order of 1-3 percentage points.
- **Wilson & Caliskan (2024, AAAI/ACM AIES):** resume-screening via LLM retrieval showed race, gender, and intersectional bias favoring White and male-associated names.
- **Fabris et al. (2024/2025, ACM TIST), the multidisciplinary survey:** the central honest conclusion is that whether algorithmic hiring is less biased and more beneficial than low-tech alternatives "currently remains unanswered."
- **Algorithmic friction / false negatives:** automated screens optimize against false positives and thereby systematically over-reject qualified candidates, an inefficiency invisible because rejected candidates get no feedback.

The precision-recall tradeoff at scale is the crux: at hundreds-to-thousands of applicants per role, a high-precision automated filter (few unqualified pass) necessarily sacrifices recall (many qualified rejected), and those false negatives concentrate adverse impact and regulatory exposure. This is why ranking is the wrong primary instrument for a two-sided match-quality goal.

### Sub-question 6: Two-sided matching framing

The decisive reframing: this is a matching market with two-sided private information and preferences, not a one-way filter. Groh et al. (2015) is the keystone evidence that even perfectly-screened matches collapse over non-wage attributes (28% interview rejection; 83% offer rejection/quick quit). Candidate preferences over schedule, location, commute, pay transparency, and speed are first-order.

The mid-funnel is where frontline matches die. The candidate-experience and ghosting literature shows that withdrawal is driven by application length, uncertainty about meeting requirements, missing salary information, and slow/absent communication. The iCIMS 2025 report's three dominant abandonment drivers were: 50% of candidates cited forms "too lengthy or time-consuming," 35% were uncertain they met requirements, and 31% left because salary was not listed. On the communication side, per Criteria Corp's 2024 candidate-experience report (n=2,516), 34% of candidates assume they have been ghosted after just one week of silence, so a two-sided design must (a) surface dealbreakers and honest job information before the application, (b) confirm mutual fit cheaply, and (c) keep the candidate informed to prevent avoidable drop-off. Pay-range transparency is one of the highest-yield levers: per SHRM data, organizations that include pay ranges see 70% more applicants and 66% higher applicant quality (cited in iCIMS 2025 frontline hiring reporting).

### Sub-question 7: Candidate experience and equity as hard constraints

Gilliland (1993) defines the procedural-justice rules that govern applicant reactions: job-relatedness, opportunity to perform, consistency of administration, transparency/explanation, two-way communication, and propriety of questions. Hausknecht, Day & Thomas (2004, Personnel Psychology) meta-analytically link these perceptions to organizational attractiveness and intentions. Highly-digitalized/automated selection can signal lower procedural justice unless designed for transparency.

Success metrics (two-sided dashboard):
- Recruiter hours per hire and qualified-applicant yield (recruiter-side efficiency).
- Application abandonment/drop-off rate by stage, and mid-funnel ghosting (candidate-side wasted effort).
- Time-to-hire and time-to-first-response.
- Perceived procedural fairness (survey).
- Adverse-impact ratios (four-fifths rule) by protected group at every automated gate.
- Downstream: show-up rate, 30/90-day early retention, the outcomes employers actually pay for.

### Sub-question 8: The regulatory envelope

- **EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689):** recruitment, applicant screening/evaluation, and hiring decisions are high-risk under Annex III point 4. High-risk obligations (risk management, data governance, technical documentation, logging under Art. 12, human oversight, conformity assessment under Art. 43) become enforceable 2 August 2026. Deployers (employers) carry independent obligations that cannot be contracted to the vendor. Penalties up to EUR 15 million or 3% of global turnover.
- **NYC Local Law 144 (in force, enforced from 5 July 2023):** automated employment decision tools require an annual independent bias audit (selection rates and impact ratios across sex, race/ethnicity, and intersectional categories), public posting, and candidate notice at least 10 business days in advance, with an opt-out/alternative-process request. A December 2025 NY State Comptroller audit found enforcement had been ineffective, signalling likely tightening.
- **UK GDPR Article 22 / Data (Use and Access) Act 2025:** Article 22 established a general prohibition on solely-automated decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects ("The data subject shall have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing... which produces legal effects concerning him or her or similarly significantly affects him or her"), with exceptions (contractual necessity, legal authorization, explicit consent) and mandatory safeguards (information, human intervention, right to contest). Section 80 of the DUAA 2025 replaces Article 22 with new Articles 22A-D, narrowing the blanket prohibition mainly to special-category-data decisions while requiring safeguards for other automated decisions; commencement is staged. The ICO test for "solely automated" turns on "meaningful human involvement", a reviewer who can change the decision takes the processing outside the Article 22 rules.
- **UK Equality Act 2010:** s.13 (direct discrimination, "because of a protected characteristic, A treats B less favourably") and s.19 (indirect discrimination, "a provision, criterion or practice which is discriminatory", i.e., a PCP causing group disadvantage unless a proportionate means of a legitimate aim) both catch algorithmic screens. The new s.19A (in force 1 January 2024) extends indirect discrimination to claimants who suffer "substantively the same disadvantage" without personally holding the characteristic, widening the pool of potential claimants. Protected characteristics are the nine listed under s.4. The EHRC has named the use of AI in recruitment a focus area and warns that algorithmic bias can produce less favourable treatment on grounds such as race and sex.
- **ICO AI-in-recruitment audit (6 November 2024):** consensual audits of AI recruitment tool providers (Aug 2023-May 2024) yielded almost 300 recommendations distilled into 7 key recommendation areas (fairness; data minimisation/purpose limitation; lawful basis and special-category data; transparency/explainability; human oversight; DPIAs; controller/processor roles). Findings: some tools allowed recruiters to filter candidates on protected characteristics; some inferred gender/ethnicity from candidate names rather than asking; "being 'at least better than random'" accuracy "would usually not be sufficient to comply" for automated decisions; data minimisation and controller/processor classification failures were common.

The regulatory gradient is clear and reinforces the evidence: transparent, candidate-driven self-selection and self-reported eligibility/availability questions are the lowest-risk design; opaque algorithmic ranking/scoring is the highest-risk design and triggers the heaviest compliance load. The UK, EU, and NYC regimes differ in form (UK = principles-based data-protection plus Equality Act; EU = comprehensive high-risk product-safety/conformity regime; NYC = narrow mandatory bias-audit plus transparency) but all push toward human oversight, transparency, and disparate-impact testing.

### What the commercial market is doing, and whether it works

Conversational pre-screening and apply flows (Paradox/Olivia, Fountain, XOR, Phenom, SmartRecruiters, plus newer entrants like Carv) dominate frontline hiring. The consistent, independently-corroborated claim is that they reduce time-to-hire, recruiter-coordinator workload, and scheduling back-and-forth, and that they improve completion via mobile-first, low-friction flows. Vendor case studies report large hire-rate, completion, and time-to-hire gains (e.g., the Paradox/Chipotle "apron on" reduction from 12 to 4 days and completion lift from 50% to over 85%; Paradox-cited GM recruiter-time savings; Johnson Controls hire-rate lift), but these are vendor-reported throughput/speed/completion metrics. There is little independent evidence that these tools improve two-sided MATCH quality (better show-up, retention, performance, lower mutual wasted effort) rather than simply processing the same pool faster. Independent reviewers explicitly note the absence of public hard-number case studies and that the tools are built for speed and logistics, not selection validity. Net assessment: the market optimizes the recruiter-side throughput metric because it is sellable and measurable, leaving the match-quality problem (the candidate-side information/incentive problem) comparatively under-served, which is precisely inploi's opportunity.

### The three core design tradeoffs (assessment)

(a) **Friction.** Removing friction maximizes reach but lets unsuitable volume through; adding friction filters intent but deters strong candidates with options. Resolution: only decision-relevant friction (availability, shift, location, genuine-interest, short SJT) is informative; generic friction is purely deterrent. Calibrate to signal, not effort.

(b) **Algorithmic ranking.** Scales but carries the worst combined risk: false negatives, disparate impact, regulatory exposure (EU AI Act high-risk, LL144 audits, UK Art. 22 successor provisions / Equality Act). Use it, if at all, only as decision-support behind meaningful human review, never as an automated rejection gate.

(c) **Honest self-selection via RJPs.** Lowest-risk, cleanest causal evidence, but modest documented effect sizes (turnover r about -.07) from mostly static previews. The upside: effect sizes were measured on passive previews; interactive, personalized previews that force a fit decision are the plausible route to larger effects and are largely untested, an evidence gap inploi can fill.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 (highest priority, lowest risk): Build the candidate-side honest self-selection layer.** Combine (i) a specific, balanced, realistic preview of the actual role (real shift patterns, pace, physical demands, pay range, location) delivered in interactive/video form, with (ii) a short set of decision-relevant self-qualification questions focused on the predictive axes, availability/shift fit, location/commute, genuine interest, and right-to-work, NOT formal experience. Mechanism: raises perceived honesty (Earnest et al. 2011) and triggers self-selection, reducing both recruiter review burden and candidate wasted effort. Benchmark to change course: if qualified-applicant yield rises and abandonment among qualified candidates does not, expand; if abandonment among qualified candidates rises, the questions have become generic friction, cut them back.

**Stage 2: Strip non-informative friction and add transparency.** Remove logins (account creation alone drives 40% higher abandonment per Appcast), CV re-keying, and redundant fields; keep the core flow under 5 minutes (12.47% vs 3.61% completion versus 15-minute-plus flows); publish pay ranges and expected timeline up front (70% more applicants and 66% higher quality per SHRM); keep candidates informed to prevent mid-funnel ghosting (34% assume rejection after one week of silence). Benchmark: completion rate and time-to-first-response.

**Stage 3: Use light structured signals for the dispositional predictors, behind human review.** Where additional screening is justified, prefer short situational-judgment / biodata items (validity about .44 for empirically-keyed biodata; conscientiousness strongest in routinized roles) over experience filters or opaque CV scoring. Keep any algorithmic output as decision-support with meaningful human review (required by UK Art. 22 successor provisions and consistent with EU AI Act human-oversight duties).

**Stage 4: Instrument the two-sided dashboard and run experiments.** Track recruiter hours per hire, qualified-applicant yield, stage-level drop-off, time-to-hire, procedural-fairness survey scores, four-fifths adverse-impact ratios at every gate, and downstream show-up and 30/90-day retention. A/B test interactive vs static previews to generate the proprietary causal evidence the field lacks. Benchmark to escalate to any automated scoring: only if it demonstrably improves recall without breaching four-fifths and passes an independent bias audit.

**What would change these recommendations:** If A/B tests show interactive previews do NOT lift self-selection beyond static ones, lean harder on pay/schedule transparency and friction removal. If an automated ranking tool can demonstrate, in independent audit, both higher recall than human triage and adverse-impact ratios above 0.8 across intersectional groups, it can be promoted from decision-support toward a heavier role, but only with the EU AI Act / LL144 / UK compliance scaffolding in place.

## Caveats
- RJP effect sizes are modest and most were measured on static previews and largely on post-hire turnover; the self-selection (pre-hire withdrawal) effect specifically is less precisely quantified than the overall turnover effect. The claim that interactive previews outperform static ones is theoretically well-grounded but not yet established by strong field experiments.
- Much of the frontline abandonment/ghosting data comes from vendor and industry surveys (iCIMS, SHRM, Appcast, Greenhouse, iHire, Criteria Corp), not peer-reviewed studies; treat specific percentages as directional.
- Vendor case-study numbers (Paradox, Fountain) are self-reported and measure throughput/speed/completion, not match quality; independent evidence is thin.
- The Groh et al. evidence is from a developing-country graduate context, not Western frontline hiring; the mechanism (non-wage preferences dominating) generalizes well, but the magnitudes may not.
- LLM-bias findings are recent and model-version-specific; direction and magnitude of bias shift across models and updates, which itself is an argument against relying on them for automated decisions.
- The UK DUAA 2025 reforms to Article 22 were recent at the time of writing with staged commencement; exact in-force dates of Articles 22A-D should be confirmed against current ICO guidance.
